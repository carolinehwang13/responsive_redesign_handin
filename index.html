Part 1: Indentifying Usability Problems

Picking a Webpage

The webpage I chose to redesign is Norby's Cafe. I chose this website because it's for a cafe in my hometown that I go to with my family all the time, but their website is not very efficient and does not represent the cozy vibes that you get going in-person. Finding Problems

This table presents some of the my findings on the good vs bad usability I noted for their current webpage.

Good Usability Bad Usability
  • Good color contrast on main page
  • Images have alt text
  • Linear layout, not too cluttered
  • HTML seems organized structurally (from what I can see)
  • Layout is just one long scroll — could be difficult to find specific sections of menu because you have to scroll all the way down and look for it
  • It is not clear that the items on the menu section are links to the online ordering website
  • Hard to tell where to go to online order — bad learnability
  • The neon yellow background could overwhelm users
  • Text on menu section has bad contrast (yellow on white)
  • Some items have price, some do not
  • Difficult to see which price aligns with which item
  • Columns on menu section are misaligned at some points
  • There is a search bar, but it doesn’t do anything
  • There is a section that I think is trying to show it on Google Maps, but I always only see a “Google Maps platform rejected your request” error
  • It looks a little odd on larger screens because the content does not fill them
  • Photos have a slightly disorienting border
  • Even though the website does what it needs to do, I think it could be made more overall efficient, appealing, and informative

Accessibility

To get a general idea of the accessibility, I put the website into WebAIM WAVE. The only error that WebAIM WAVE gave me was a “missing form label”. It also gave some alerts like “redundant link”, “skipped heading level”, and “justified text”. I agreed with the WebAIM WAVE report about the accessibility problems it pointed out. However, because since it is such a straightforward website (basically just text and images), it does not have many chances for it to go astray. I think that WebAIM WAVE is good at detecting accessibility issues when it comes to design choices that are represented in the code (like alt text, colors, etc.), but it doesn’t necessarily account for more subjective design choices, like layouts, which I believe are a key aspect I sought to address in my redesign.
